snakemake -n \--use-conda \--cores 8

snakemake \--use-conda \--cores 8

**1. Introduction**
===================

Mosquito-borne diseases remain among the most significant threats to
global public health, with *Culex* mosquitoes serving as primary vectors
for several neurotropic arboviruses and filarial nematodes. The *Culex
pipiens* species complex, which includes *Cx. pipiens* (with recognized
forms *pipiens*, *molestus*, and *pallens*) and *Cx. quinquefasciatus*,
represents the most widely distributed and medically important group
within the genus *Culex* (Farajollahi et al., 2011). Members of this
complex are the principal vectors of West Nile virus (WNV), St. Louis
encephalitis virus (SLEV), Japanese encephalitis virus (JEV), and
*Wuchereria bancrofti*, the causative agent of lymphatic filariasis. The
complex also includes *Cx. australicus* and *Cx. globocoxitus*, which
are restricted to Australia (Aardema et al., 2022), but no genome
assemblies were publicly available for these taxa at the time of
analysis.

Despite their close evolutionary relationships and the ability to
produce fertile hybrids in laboratory settings, the forms within the
*Cx. pipiens* complex exhibit striking ecological and behavioral
differences that directly influence disease transmission dynamics
(Farajollahi et al., 2011). *Culex pipiens* f. *pipiens* is an obligate
blood-feeder that requires a blood meal before egg production
(anautogeny), undergoes reproductive diapause in winter, and
preferentially feeds on avian hosts, making it a critical bridge vector
for WNV transmission from birds to mammals. In contrast, *Cx. pipiens*
f. *molestus* is capable of producing its first egg batch without a
blood meal (autogeny), does not enter diapause, preferentially feeds on
mammals including humans, and thrives in underground habitats such as
subway tunnels and basement water collections. *Culex pipiens* f.
*pallens* is distributed primarily in East Asia and exhibits
intermediate characteristics between *pipiens* and *molestus*, likely
reflecting a history of hybridization (Aardema et al., 2020). *Culex
quinquefasciatus*, the southern house mosquito, dominates tropical and
subtropical regions and is a major vector of lymphatic filariasis and
WNV in the Americas.

The genomic basis for these phenotypic differences remains largely
unresolved. Previous comparative genomic studies of *Culex* mosquitoes
have primarily focused on individual species or pairwise comparisons
(Aardema et al., 2020; Aardema et al., 2022), limiting our understanding
of the full spectrum of genetic variation within the complex. The
pangenome framework, which partitions the total gene repertoire of a
species or group into core genes shared by all members, shell genes
shared by subsets, and cloud genes unique to individual members, offers
a powerful approach for characterizing gene content variation in closely
related organisms (Golicz et al., 2020). Although pangenome analyses
have transformed our understanding of genome evolution in bacteria and
plants, their application in insects remains limited. Recent pangenome
studies in the silkworm *Bombyx mori* (Tong et al., 2022) and the fall
armyworm *Spodoptera frugiperda* (Huang et al., 2025) have demonstrated
the utility of this framework for revealing gene content variation,
structural dynamics, and signatures of selection in insect genomes.
However, no pangenome analysis has yet been conducted for any mosquito
species, despite the medical importance and taxonomic complexity of
groups such as the *Cx. pipiens* complex. The closest analog in an
insect disease vector comes from the *Anopheles gambiae* sensu lato
species complex, where whole-genome comparison across six sibling
species has resolved species-level relationships and demonstrated
pervasive autosomal introgression between the principal human malaria
vectors (Fontaine et al., 2015; Neafsey et al., 2015). No comparable
analysis has been reported for the *Cx. pipiens* complex, despite its
comparable medical importance and the recent availability of
chromosome-scale reference assemblies for four of its major forms.

Here, we present the first pangenome analysis of the *Cx. pipiens*
species complex. We integrate four publicly available chromosome-level
genome assemblies representing the major forms within the complex, along
with *Cx. tarsalis* as an outgroup. We characterize the pangenome
architecture, reconstruct phylogenomic relationships, identify gene
families undergoing significant evolutionary change, compare
transposable element landscapes, and assess whole-genome synteny across
all pairwise combinations. Our results reveal substantial gene content
variation among closely related mosquito forms and provide a genomic
framework for understanding the ecological and behavioral divergence
within this medically important species complex.

**2. Materials and Methods** 
============================

**2.1 Genome Assemblies**
-------------------------

Four chromosome-scale genome assemblies representing members of the
*Culex pipiens* Four chromosome-scale genome assemblies representing
members of the *Culex pipiens* species complex were retrieved from NCBI
GenBank for pangenome analysis (Table 1). These included *Cx.
quinquefasciatus* (GCF\_015732765.1; 578 Mb), *Cx. pipiens* f. *pallens*
(GCF\_016801865.2; 568 Mb), *Cx. pipiens* f. *molestus*
(GCA\_024516115.1; 560 Mb), and *Cx. pipiens* f. *pipiens*
(GCA\_963924435.1; 533 Mb). *Cx. tarsalis* (GCA\_016859205.1; 50 Mb) was
included as a phylogenetic outgroup for tree rooting and was excluded
from pangenome partitioning. The *Cx. pipiens* complex also includes
*Cx. australicus* and *Cx. globocoxitus* (Aardema et al., 2020; Aardema
et al., 2022), but no chromosome-scale genome assembly has been
deposited in a public database for either taxon. Previous phylogenetic
work using targeted sequencing places these two Australian endemics as a
monophyletic sister clade to *Cx. quinquefasciatus*, with a relatively
older divergence than the separations among the four taxa analyzed here
(Aardema et al., 2020). The absence of reference assemblies for these
lineages is therefore a shared limitation of the current field and is
addressed further in the Discussion.

Genome selection was governed by three criteria: (i) assembly contiguity
at the chromosome or near-chromosome scale, which is required for
meaningful whole-genome synteny analysis; (ii) BUSCO completeness ≥ 95%
against the diptera\_odb10 reference set, ensuring that pangenome
partitioning is not biased by assembly fragmentation; and (iii)
availability through NCBI GenBank to support reproducibility. The four
assemblies included here represent the complete set of genomes within
the *Cx. pipiens* complex that satisfy all three criteria as of the
analysis date. We explicitly avoided supplementing this panel with
short-read draft assemblies, which frequently underrepresent repetitive
and gene-dense regions and would introduce a systematic confound in
pangenome partitioning: apparent shell or cloud orthogroups in such
assemblies often reflect assembly gaps rather than true gene content
variation.

*Cx. quinquefasciatus* GCF\_015732765.1 was selected as the reference
genome because it is the only assembly in the complex with a full NCBI
RefSeq annotation, providing a curated set of gene models for downstream
annotation transfer. This assembly also represents the most extensively
studied member of the complex in terms of genomic resources and has
served as the primary reference in prior comparative analyses of the
group (Arensburger et al., 2010; Ryazansky et al., 2024).

**2.2 Annotation Transfer**
---------------------------

Gene models were transferred from the *Cx. quinquefasciatus* reference
annotation (NCBI RefSeq) to the three remaining ingroup assemblies (*Cx.
pipiens* f. *pallens*, f. *molestus*, and f. *pipiens*) and the *Cx.
tarsalis* outgroup using Liftoff v1.6.3 \[Shumate and Salzberg 2021
ref\]. Liftoff maps reference gene features to target assemblies through
whole-genome alignment, preserving gene structure including exon-intron
boundaries. Default parameters were used for all transfers.

Protein sequences were extracted from each annotated genome using
gffread v0.12.7 \[Pertea and Pertea 2020 ref\] with the -y flag to
translate coding sequences from the transferred GFF3 annotations. The
*Cx. quinquefasciatus* reference proteins were extracted directly from
its RefSeq annotation. This produced 24,199 proteins for *Cx.
quinquefasciatus*, 23,174 for *Cx. pipiens* f. *pipiens*, 22,585 for
*Cx. pipiens* f. *pallens*, and 22,582 for *Cx. pipiens* f. *molestus*.
The variation in protein count across assemblies likely reflects
differences in assembly completeness and the proportion of reference
gene models that could be successfully lifted over to each target
genome. Extracted protein sets served as input for orthology inference.

The single copy orthologous genes (SCOs) were collected from the results
generated by OrthoFinder v2.5.5. Orthogroups with one ortholog for each
of the four ingroup genomes were obtained and sequences smaller than 50
amino acids were eliminated to minimize noise caused by weakly conserved
and fragmented genes. After filtering, a total of 8,616 SCOs were
included.

For each SCO, protein sequences were aligned independently with MAFFT
v7.520 (default options). Aligned sequences were trimmed using trimAl
v1.4.1 with option -automated1 in order to minimize spurious regions
while keeping informative alignment sites. Trimming resulted in a total
of 8,616 alignments which were then concatenated into a supermatrix.

Maximum likelihood trees were inferred with IQ-TREE v2.2.6. Best fitting
model of amino acid substitutions was chosen through the use of
ModelFinder (-m MFP) option. Branch support was determined with the help
of ultrafast bootstrapping (-bb 1000) with 1,000 iterations. In
addition, gene concordance factors (gCF) and site concordance factors
(sCF) for all inner nodes were obtained for better phylogenetic
assessment.

**2.4 Phylogenomic Analysis**
-----------------------------

*\[Describe SCO extraction from OrthoFinder single-copy orthogroups.
Alignment with MAFFT v7.520, trimming with trimAl v1.4.1 (-automated1).

The evolution of gene families was investigated with the CAFE5 package
v5.1.0 to find significantly expanded or contracted families across the
phylogeny. The number of genes per orthogroup calculated with
OrthoFinder served as an input and reflected the size of each gene
family within the four ingroup species. The maximum likelihood tree
built with IQ-TREE was employed as a guide tree for all calculations.

Gamma distribution for the gene family evolutionary rates was utilized
with three rate classes (k = 3). Thus, different rates could be
estimated for each gene family rather than one uniform rate for all of
them. The birth-death rate parameter (λ) was estimated globally, which
describes the frequency of gains and losses of gene copies in gene
families. Also, the rate shape parameter (α) was estimated from the
data. Gene families with p-values \< 0.05 were considered significantly
expanded or contracted.

**2.6 Whole-Genome Synteny and ANI**
------------------------------------

Pairwise whole-genome alignments were performed among the four ingroup
genomes to assess synteny on a large-scale and the similarity of the
sequences. Genome comparisons were conducted using the nucmer program
from MUMmer v4.0.0. The alignments were then filtered using delta-filter
with parameters of -l 1000 -q, in order to retain high-confidence
alignments of at least 1,000 base pairs. This enables prioritization of
the best query alignments. Alignment coordinates and percent identity
statistics were then extracted with show-coords.

The average nucleotide identity (ANI) values were calculated for each
pairwise comparison, as alignment-length-weighted mean percent identity
across all retained alignment blocks. This included six pairwise genome
comparisons which were performed amongst four ingroup genomes. The ANI
values were then summarized in an ANI matrix, shown in Table 2.

**2.7 Transposable Element Analysis**
-------------------------------------

Transposable element (TE) content was then characterized for each genome
through de novo repeat library construction, using RepeatModeler v2.0.7
for each genome. Species-specific repeat libraries were first generated
independently for each genome, then the custom repeat libraries were
used in order to annotate the repetitive elements. This genome
annotation was performed with RepeatMasker v4.1.7, using the
species-specific libraries to identify and classify interspersed repeats
and other repetitive elements throughout each genome assembly. As a
result, the interspersed repeat content and total masked content was
calculated, to be discussed in the results section.

**2.8 Anvi\'o Pangenome Visualization**
---------------------------------------

An independent pangenomic analysis was conducted using anvi\'o v8 \[Eren
et al. 2021 ref\] to provide a complementary gene cluster level view of
the pangenome and generate an interactive visualization. Contigs
databases were created for each of the four ingroup genomes using
anvi-gen-contigs-database with external gene calls derived from the
Liftoff transferred annotations. A genomes storage database was then
constructed to consolidate all four genomes. The pangenomic workflow was
executed using anvi-pan-genome, which performed an all vs all amino acid
sequence similarity search with DIAMOND across 68,043 combined protein
sequences. Weak hits were removed using a minbit heuristic threshold of
0.5, which filters reciprocal DIAMOND matches based on the ratio of
actual bitscore to maximum possible bitscore, retaining only pairs where
both proteins show substantial mutual coverage. Filtered hits were then
clustered into gene clusters using the MCL algorithm with an inflation
parameter of 10. The phylogenomic species tree inferred from IQ-TREE was
imported as a genome layer order, and the pangenome was visualized
interactively using anvi-display-pan. The final visualization was
exported as SVG and refined for publication.

Phylogenomic analysis based on 8,616 single-copy orthologues produced a

contracted. A similar yet slightly less extreme pattern was observed in

**4. Discussion**
=================

+----------------------------------------------------------------------+
| **ASSIGNED TO: TYLER**                                               |
|                                                                      |
| Sections: Section 4                                                  |
|                                                                      |
| I will write this after everyone else is done. Synthesis and         |
| interpretation, 4-6 paragraphs.                                      |
+----------------------------------------------------------------------+

Here we report the first pangenome characterization of the *Culex
pipiens* species complex and use it to refine, rather than simply
redescribe, the genomic relationships among its members. Three findings
reshape the current picture of this group. First, at the nucleotide
level the four forms are remarkably similar (pairwise ANI 97.9--98.3%),
yet they partition 92,540 genes into 11,653 core orthogroups, 4,205
shell orthogroups, and 511 cloud orthogroups, with form-specific cloud
content distributed unevenly across the four taxa (χ² = 85.36, df = 3, p
\< 2 × 10⁻¹⁶). High nucleotide identity therefore masks substantial
gene-content and structural divergence---a pattern the synteny dot plots
make visually obvious, particularly for comparisons involving *Cx.
quinquefasciatus*. Second, the phylogenomic supermatrix places *Cx.
pallens* and *Cx. pipiens* as sister taxa with *Cx. molestus* as the
next outgroup and *Cx. quinquefasciatus* as the complex-internal
outgroup, with complete concordance across all 8,616 gene trees at every
node. This arrangement is consistent with the hybrid-origin hypothesis
for *Cx. pallens* proposed on the basis of population genomic data
(Aardema et al., 2020): under that model, *Cx. pallens* would be
expected to share substantial ancestry with both *Cx. pipiens* and *Cx.
quinquefasciatus*, and its placement as sister to *Cx. pipiens* in the
autosomal supermatrix is compatible with that inheritance pattern.
Third, every terminal branch shows a significant excess of gene family
expansions over contractions (binomial p \< 2 × 10⁻¹⁶ in each case),
with *Cx. quinquefasciatus* showing the strongest expansion bias (3,671
expansions vs. 866 contractions), indicating that lineage-specific gene
family turnover---not genome-wide sequence divergence---is the primary
axis of functional differentiation within this young complex.

**4.1 Rationale for a four-genome design**
------------------------------------------

A four-genome panel is, by the standards of bacterial or plant
pangenomics, a small design. We adopted it deliberately rather than
expansively, and the rationale is worth making explicit because it
shapes the interpretive weight of every result that follows. First, the
panel includes every chromosome-scale *Cx. pipiens* complex genome
currently available; it is not a subsampled convenience set. Second, the
*Cx. pipiens* complex is a young radiation: the ANI values of
97.9--98.3% recovered here are consistent with divergences within the
last several million years, and the four taxa analyzed are all capable
of producing fertile hybrids in the laboratory (Farajollahi et al.,
2011). At this level of divergence, a small high-contiguity panel is
more informative than a larger panel of mixed-quality assemblies:
pangenome partitioning is exquisitely sensitive to missing sequence, and
a single fragmented short-read assembly can inflate the shell and cloud
compartments by tens of percent. Third, the four forms included here
span the full axis of ecological and behavioral divergence documented
within the complex---tropical versus temperate distribution, autogenous
versus anautogenous reproduction, diapausing versus non-diapausing, and
ornithophilic versus mammophilic host preference (Farajollahi et al.,
2011). A four-genome pangenome therefore still resolves the biological
contrasts that motivate the study, even while we acknowledge that
replicate genomes per form would allow within-form variation to be
separated from between-form divergence.

The two omitted complex members, *Cx. australicus* and *Cx.
globocoxitus*, are Australian endemics that sit as a monophyletic group
sister to *Cx. quinquefasciatus* in published phylogenies, with a deeper
divergence than the splits among the four forms analyzed here (Aardema
et al., 2020). Their inclusion would meaningfully extend the pangenome
in two directions: it would add an older internal branch against which
to calibrate gene family turnover in CAFE5, and it would allow the core
compartment to be defined across a broader evolutionary window. The
limiting factor is purely one of genomic resources---no chromosome-scale
assembly has yet been published for either species, and the taxa are
geographically restricted in a way that has not yet drawn the sequencing
investment that *Cx. pipiens* s.s., *Cx. quinquefasciatus*, *Cx.
pallens*, and *Cx. molestus* have all received. A second-generation
pangenome of the *Cx. pipiens* complex incorporating chromosome-scale
assemblies of *Cx. australicus* and *Cx. globocoxitus* is a natural next
step for the community.

**4.2 Comparison with other insect vector complexes**
-----------------------------------------------------

Our analytical framework parallels, but does not duplicate, recent work
on the *Anopheles gambiae* species complex. Fontaine et al. (2015) used
chromosome-scale reference assemblies for six sibling species in that
complex to resolve a contested species tree and to demonstrate pervasive
autosomal introgression among vectors, recovering a topology in which
the most competent human malaria vectors are not sister taxa. That
study's central methodological insight---that a small panel of
high-contiguity genomes within a single young radiation is sufficient to
resolve species-level relationships and detect reticulation---is
directly applicable here. The *Cx. pipiens* complex has no comparable
published phylogenomic treatment, and the Fontaine et al. framework
represents the closest methodological analog to the present work among
insect disease vectors.

The broader comparative context comes from Neafsey et al. (2015), who
sequenced sixteen anopheline genomes spanning \~100 million years of
divergence and reported accelerated rates of gene gain and loss,
elevated gene shuffling, and faster intron turnover in *Anopheles*
relative to *Drosophila*. Our CAFE5 result---every terminal branch of
the *Cx. pipiens* complex showing a significant excess of gene family
expansions over contractions, with 3,050--3,671 expansion events per
lineage---is consistent with this broader pattern of gene family
dynamism in mosquitoes, and suggests that the dynamism previously
documented across deep anopheline divergences extends to shallow
divergences within a single culicine complex.

It is worth noting that small-panel pangenome analyses using
core/shell/cloud partitioning are well-established in bacterial and
plant systems (Tettelin et al., 2005; Golicz et al., 2020), but have not
previously been applied to any mosquito species complex and remain
uncommon in animal comparative genomics more generally. The analytical
choices made here---OrthoFinder with DIAMOND for sequence similarity,
MCL clustering at inflation 1.5, and strict core/shell/cloud
partitioning at the 4/2--3/1 genome thresholds---follow standard
bacterial pangenomics conventions. This choice is deliberate:
hierarchical orthologous group frameworks typical of drosophilid
comparative work are optimized for inferring gene evolutionary histories
across deeper divergences, whereas gene-presence/absence pangenome
partitioning is better suited to characterizing the shallow,
recently-diverged radiation represented by the *Cx. pipiens* complex.

**4.3 Transposable element landscape and genome-size conservation**
-------------------------------------------------------------------

The remarkable conservation of transposable element content across the
four genomes (52.14--53.91% interspersed repeats) contrasts with the
considerable variation in predicted protein counts (22,582--24,199) and
in pangenome compartment membership. TE content at this level is typical
of culicine genomes and suggests that the overall repeat architecture
was largely established before the radiation of the complex and has not
diverged substantially among forms. By implication, the observed
differences in gene-content compartments and gene-family sizes are not
readily attributable to global shifts in genome-wide TE activity,
although fine-scale TE-mediated gene duplication or disruption at
individual loci cannot be excluded from the present whole-genome
analysis.

**4.4 Limitations and future directions**
-----------------------------------------

Three limitations of the present design bound the inferences that can be
drawn. First, a single chromosome-scale assembly is currently available
for each of the four forms, so within-form population-level variation is
not captured; the cloud and shell compartments reported here represent
between-form differences only, and intraspecific gene presence/absence
variation is expected to contribute additional pangenome content that
future resequencing panels will need to characterize. Second, annotation
transfer via Liftoff preserves the gene models defined in the *Cx.
quinquefasciatus* reference; form-specific genes absent from that
reference would not be recovered by this approach and may be partly
responsible for the comparatively small cloud compartment of the
reference genome itself. De novo annotation of each genome, followed by
cross-validation of gene models, would refine the cloud estimate. Third,
as discussed above, the exclusion of *Cx. australicus* and *Cx.
globocoxitus* leaves the deepest divergence within the complex
unsampled.

The *Cx. pipiens* complex therefore joins the *An. gambiae* complex as
one of the few medically-important mosquito species complexes with a
chromosome-scale comparative genomic framework. Where the *An. gambiae*
work revealed that vector-competence traits can be acquired through
interspecific introgression rather than solely through de novo mutation
(Fontaine et al., 2015), our results suggest a complementary pattern in
the *Cx. pipiens* complex: substantial lineage-specific gene family
turnover layered on top of near-identical nucleotide backgrounds.
Whether the 511 cloud orthogroups partitioned unevenly across the four
forms (*molestus* = 200, *pipiens* = 142, *pallens* = 114,
*quinquefasciatus* = 55) contribute to the ecological and behavioral
differences that distinguish these forms---autogeny, diapause, host
preference, habitat specificity---is a question that follow-up
functional analyses and population-scale resequencing will be needed to
resolve.

**5. Conclusions**
==================

| Brief concluding paragraph. |
+-----------------------------+

We present the first pangenome characterization of the *Culex pipiens*
species complex, integrating four chromosome-scale genome assemblies to
recover 11,653 core, 4,205 shell, and 511 cloud orthogroups. Despite
near-uniform nucleotide identity (97.9--98.3% ANI) and a conserved TE
architecture, the four forms differ substantially in gene-content
composition, synteny structure, and lineage-specific gene-family
turnover, with *Cx. quinquefasciatus* showing the strongest expansion
bias and *Cx. molestus* carrying the most form-specific genes.
Phylogenomic analysis of 8,616 single-copy orthologs resolves *Cx.
pallens* and *Cx. pipiens* as sister taxa under a topology compatible
with the hybrid-origin hypothesis for *Cx. pallens*. These results
establish a genomic framework for the complex that complements the
comparable phylogenomic treatment of the *Anopheles gambiae* sibling
species complex, and they lay the groundwork for functional follow-up
linking the uneven distribution of form-specific gene content to the
ecological and behavioral traits that make these mosquitoes clinically
distinct vectors.
